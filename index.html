Traffic Levels

📈

Growth vs. Level

The growth-rate correlation with 5G penetration is weak (r = −0.05), but the level correlation is 0.48 — reflecting a market maturity effect, not a 5G failure.

Deep Dive: What the Data Shows

Research Question 1

How much more data do high-5G markets consume?

Country-years with at least 20% estimated 5G penetration consume on average 2.8 times more data per subscription per month than country-years below 5% penetration. North America leads with an estimated 5G penetration of 68% and an average of 11.9 GB/sub/month in 2024, while Sub-Saharan Africa sits at 2.6% penetration and just 4.5 GB/sub/month — less than half the global average.

Importantly, this ratio reflects country-level market conditions rather than individual subscriber behaviour. High-5G markets also tend to have higher income, better fixed infrastructure, and more mature content ecosystems, all of which independently raise traffic levels.

Research Question 2

Does 5G penetration predict faster traffic growth?

The answer is nuanced. The correlation between estimated 5G penetration and year-over-year traffic growth rate is −0.05 — essentially zero and slightly negative. This reflects a market maturity effect: high-5G countries already have very high traffic levels, so their percentage growth appears slower. Countries with low 5G penetration show high percentage growth because they are growing from a much smaller base.

However, the level correlation between 5G penetration and absolute traffic per subscription is 0.48 — a meaningful positive association. This confirms that 5G markets consume substantially more data in absolute terms, even if their growth rates look modest.

Research Question 3

When will 5G become the dominant traffic source?

Using an Ericsson Mobility Report-calibrated traffic-share trajectory, 5G traffic is estimated to surpass 4G as the dominant source of mobile data around 2026. This projection uses industry-level traffic share anchors rather than the ITU dataset directly, because ITU country-year exports do not split traffic by network generation.

This timeline varies significantly by region. East Asia and North America are likely to cross the 50% 5G traffic threshold earlier, while Sub-Saharan Africa and Latin America may not reach this point until well into the 2030s given current rollout trajectories.

Research Question 4

What drives mobile traffic — is 5G the main factor?

The Random Forest model identifies estimated 5G penetration rate as the single most important predictor (importance = 0.31), followed by mobile broadband penetration (0.20), log GDP per capita (0.20), and urbanization rate (0.16). Region and year contribute a smaller share.

This suggests that 5G does not operate in isolation. It is bundled with income growth, urbanization, and infrastructure development. Countries that deploy 5G tend to also have the economic conditions that independently drive higher data consumption. Disentangling the pure 5G effect from these correlated factors would require individual subscriber-level data, which was not available for this project.

The Random Forest (R² = 0.48, RMSE = 4.47) consistently outperformed XGBoost (R² = 0.41, RMSE = 4.77), suggesting meaningful non-linear interactions between 5G penetration and income level that tree ensembles capture better.

Methods Snapshot

The cleaned dataset merges ITU DataHub mobile-broadband traffic, mobile-broadband subscriptions, mobile-cellular subscriptions, and mobile population coverage indicators with World Bank GDP per capita and urbanization data. Country-level 5G subscription counts were not available in the local ITU exports, so the project uses an explicitly flagged synthetic estimate calibrated from observed ITU 5G population coverage, year, and region.

A tuned Random Forest model and an XGBoost comparison model predict monthly GB per active mobile-broadband subscription using estimated 5G penetration, mobile broadband penetration, log GDP per capita, urbanization, region, and year.

Limitations & Future Work

🔬 Synthetic 5G Data

Country-level 5G subscription counts were not available from ITU exports. The 5G penetration variable is a synthetic estimate derived from coverage data and region-year adoption factors. Results should be interpreted as indicative macro patterns rather than precise causal estimates.

🌐 Ecological Study Design

Countries are the unit of observation. Associations identified here cannot be directly extrapolated to individual subscriber behaviour — a country with high 5G penetration may show high traffic for reasons unrelated to 5G adoption itself.

📊 Single Data Source

All traffic and network variables come from ITU DataHub, which may have reporting gaps for lower-income countries. The dataset excludes traffic that transits private peering points and may undercount some regions.

🔭 Future Directions

Future work could incorporate GSMA Intelligence or Ericsson Mobility Visualizer data for observed 5G subscription counts, add operator-level pricing and spectrum data as features, and extend the analysis to 2025–2026 as 5G approaches traffic dominance globally.
